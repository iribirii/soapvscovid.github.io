Simulation 1

In this simulation you can visualize how soap molecules are getting attatched to the viruses placed on the surface. Those soap molecules help to take the virus off the surface. But these viruses are not destroyed yet, they remain floating in the water.

Play around with the two buttons in the simulation. Create as many soap particles as you want untill all the viruses are surrounded by soaps and the surface is completly clean. Use the reset button to stain the surface with viruses again and restart the process.

This little game simulates what occourse when you clean a contaminated surface with soapy water nd how the soap molecules take place in it, making the process much faster and efficient.

Before getting attatched to a virus, soap molecules move freely all around available space. This type of random movement of particles in a fluid is called brownian movement and it is produced due to the collisioins between these particles and the molecules forming the fluid. In this particular case, water molecules. Once a soap molecule finds a virus it gets attatched to its surface and it loses its free movement. On the other hand, once soap starts interacting with the viruses, those start moving and getting solvated.

If you would like to learn more about the process and the science behind it go to our previous post. If you want to keep fighting viruses and completly destroy them, go to the next simulation, where we will zoom in a little bit and destroy the virus into pieces.
